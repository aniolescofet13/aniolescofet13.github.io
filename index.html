About Me

Growing up in Catalonia, I was the kind of kid who genuinely could not sit still. Every tree was something to climb, every rooftop a challenge worth taking. Sports were a constant — anything physical, anything with a bit of edge. That energy never really went away. It just found better places to go.

But alongside all of that, there was always a pull towards physics, maths and understanding how things work. Not as a school obligation, but as genuine curiosity. When I figured out that engineering was where those two worlds collide, and that motorsport was where engineering gets pushed to its absolute limit, the direction became clear.

Today I am doing two Master's degrees at the same time, one at UPC Barcelona, one at Politecnico di Milano, while working at STARK Future on electric motorcycle simulation and powertrain development. Outside of that I train in some form every day: gym, running, padel, climbing, hiking. I travel whenever I can. And I keep personal engineering projects running on the side because building things out of pure curiosity is one of the best ways I know to stay sharp.

What I am after is a motorsport or prototype project that genuinely puts me to the test. Something fast-paced, technically demanding, where the engineering work shows up directly in the results. The goal is not a title on a business card. It is the kind of project where you feel the weight of every decision you make.

PP — 01

Lap Time Simulator

Modular Vehicle Development Platform · MATLAB

A MATLAB-based lap time simulation and vehicle analysis platform designed to support a racing car's full development cycle, from early concept where almost no hardware data exists, all the way through to race-ready validation against real on-track telemetry. The tool is built around the principle that the fidelity of the simulation should match the fidelity of the information available: as the team learns more about the car, the models are upgraded in place without restructuring the workflow.

GUI overview — speed profile + track coloured by speed

The problem it solves. In motorsport development, the decisions with the biggest impact on performance — suspension geometry, aero package, powertrain architecture — are made at the very beginning of a project, when the least data is available. A tool that only works once full hardware data exists arrives too late. Equally, a tool that is too simplified fails to give useful answers once real data starts coming in. This simulator solves that by being continuously useful at every stage of development, from back-of-envelope concept studies to lap-accurate simulation validated against telemetry.

Three-level fidelity architecture. Every subsystem — Powertrain, Aerodynamics, Brakes, Tyres, Suspension, Chassis — is implemented at three independent levels of physical fidelity, selectable at runtime. L1 (concept stage) uses simple scalar parameters: constant peak power, fixed drag and lift coefficients, constant friction. Enough to produce a credible lap time estimate from a one-page spec sheet. L2 (development stage) introduces physics-based models: torque curves, brake bias and hydraulic geometry, load-sensitive tyre behaviour, lateral load transfer. L3 (validation stage) goes to full fidelity: Pacejka Magic Formula tyres fitted to measured data, full hydraulic brake model with thermal degradation, DRS and ride-height-sensitive aero, ERS energy management. Each upgrade is independent — Tyres L2 can run alongside Suspension L1 if that is all the data available. The simulator loop never knows which level is active: each subsystem exposes a clean interface and higher-fidelity models are drop-in replacements.

Subsystem tab — Tyres L3 Pacejka curves at nominal and 2× load

Layer 1: QSS spatial sweep. The spatial engine is built on the Quasi-Steady-State method, the industry standard in motorsport simulation. The car is treated as a point mass navigating a known racing line, assumed to be in steady state at every spatial point. The algorithm runs in three linked sweeps across the track distance domain. First, corner speed limits: at every point with curvature κ, the maximum lateral speed is solved iteratively, since downforce and therefore grip depend on speed itself. Second, a forward acceleration sweep propagates the speed envelope accounting for engine power, drag, rolling resistance, and remaining longitudinal grip after cornering demand is subtracted via the friction ellipse. Third, a backward braking sweep runs the same logic in reverse, propagating maximum entry speeds into each corner. Five alternating passes produce the minimum-time speed envelope: at every track point the car is at exactly one limit — traction, braking, or cornering.

QSS speed envelope — traction / braking / cornering limits coloured

Layer 2: transient temporal integration and iterative coupling. Once the spatial speed profile is converged, a second pass integrates the trajectory in the time domain, computing the states that evolve continuously along the lap. The tyre thermal model tracks compound temperature using a lumped thermal network, updating friction coefficients and slip stiffness that feed back into Layer 1. Suspension and chassis dynamics compute instantaneous ride height as a function of heave and roll, which directly modifies the aero maps used in the spatial sweep. ERS state of charge is integrated from the power demand profile, determining deployment availability corner by corner. Fuel mass drops continuously, shifting total weight and CG height. These outputs are fed back into Layer 1, which re-solves the speed profile with the updated vehicle state. The two layers iterate to convergence, typically in three to five passes, producing a speed profile that accounts for both instantaneous physics and the evolving state of the car across the full lap.

Layer 2 output — tyre temperature or ERS SoC vs lap distance

Sensitivity analysis and signal explorer. Rather than running the simulator once per configuration, the sensitivity module sweeps a parameter over a range and plots the lap time response directly, answering questions like: at what downforce level does added drag outweigh added cornering grip, what is the weight penalty per second on a technical versus a high-speed circuit, or at what CG height does lateral load transfer start to hurt more than it helps. The signal explorer provides a graphical interface to inspect every computed channel as a function of track position or time — speed, longitudinal and lateral acceleration, tyre load per axle, aerodynamic downforce, brake force distribution, engine operating point, ERS contribution — and overlays channels from different configurations to directly show where one setup gains on another. The GG diagram is included as a standard assessment of how efficiently the tyre friction circle is being used.

Sensitivity sweep — lap time vs downforce level

Real data validation loop. Once the car is running on track, the simulator's outputs are compared directly against real telemetry — GPS speed, IMU accelerations, wheel speeds, brake pressure, engine channels — aligned spatially by arc length. Systematic offsets in specific sectors reveal which subsystem model needs refinement: a braking point consistently too early points to the brake model, a corner speed consistently too high points to the lateral tyre model. This closes the loop between simulation and reality and gives each model upgrade a clear validation target.

Validation overlay — simulated vs real telemetry speed trace

GUI and software architecture. The graphical interface is built entirely in programmatic MATLAB with no external toolboxes. The track tab imports GPS or Cartesian data and displays the racing line coloured by curvature, elevation profile, and corner classification. Each subsystem has its own tab with a level selector that dynamically rebuilds the parameter table and live preview plots — torque curve for Powertrain L2, Pacejka curves at nominal and 2× load for Tyres L3. The results tab shows the speed profile, GG diagram, trajectory coloured by speed, and lap time delta versus a reference. The underlying architecture is object-oriented with clean subsystem interfaces, designed so that upgrading one module never requires touching another — the same principle that makes the tool usable at every stage of a project's life.

Results tab — GG diagram + trajectory coloured by speed

FS — 01

Model-Based Torque Vectoring

4WD Electric Vehicle · CAT16x · BCNeMotorsport

Torque Vectoring on a 4WD vehicle with one independent motor per wheel is one of the most complete chassis control problems in motorsport. The system has three layers running in sequence: a nonlinear vehicle model that represents what the car is doing, a yaw rate controller that decides how much corrective yaw moment to apply, and a torque allocator that distributes that moment across all four motors in real time. Each layer feeds the next, and the whole chain runs in the processing unit of the CAT16x during competition.

System architecture — three-layer TV pipeline diagram

The vehicle model is a Dual-Track Nonlinear Time Varying (NLTV) formulation. Unlike a simple bicycle model, it tracks all four wheels individually, accounting for lateral and longitudinal load transfer, aerodynamic downforce, Ackermann steering geometry, and a Pacejka Magic Formula tire model per wheel. Parameters vary nonlinearly with vertical load and slip angle, making it accurate both in the linear and limit handling regimes. The model was calibrated against real CAT15x telemetry from three maneuvers: Ramp Steer (96.7% fit), Skid Pad (98.8% fit), and the AutoX at Formula Student Spain 2023 (82.5% fit).

Model calibration fits — Ramp Steer / Skid Pad / AutoX telemetry vs model

The yaw rate controller generates the reference corrective yaw moment that the torque allocator then executes. It combines two components: a Feed-Forward path that uses the vehicle model to anticipate the required moment based on steering input and speed, and a PID Feed-Back path that closes the loop on the measured yaw rate error. An anti-windup mechanism (back-calculation) prevents integrator saturation in transients, and a velocity-dependent reset avoids excessive integral buildup. The combined controller tracks the yaw rate reference with average errors of 0.02% in Ramp Steer, 0.18% in Skid Pad, and 0.89% in AutoX conditions.

FF+FB controller block diagram — yaw rate reference tracking

The torque allocator translates the desired longitudinal force (from the driver throttle) and corrective yaw moment (from the controller) into four individual motor torque commands. This is posed as a multi-objective quadratic programming problem solved in real time. The optimizer respects motor torque limits, inverter limits, and the tire friction circle per wheel, while simultaneously minimizing the error on both references. Using Ackermann-corrected wheel angles, it distributes torque asymmetrically between left-right and front-rear to generate the exact yaw moment needed without sacrificing traction. Closed-loop tracking errors: Fx average 0.9%, Mz average 3.6%.

QP torque allocator — torque distribution across four wheels

The entire system was developed and tested in a Simulink and VI-Grade CarRealTime loop before deployment on the real car. VI-Grade's 14-DOF multibody model replicated the CAT15x dynamics with full suspension, tire-road interaction, and real mass distribution. Three standard maneuvers were used to stress-test each layer independently and combined. Peak lateral acceleration in the AutoX improved by +7.9% over the baseline. On the hardware side, the algorithm runs as a ROS node on the car's Processing Unit, receiving data over CAN from the VectorNav INS, wheel resolvers and suspension sensors, and sending torque commands to two Lenze inverters at real-time rate.

VI-Grade HIL simulation — maneuver results and lateral acceleration gain

A key design decision was making the system fully configurable in real time. Three driving modes were defined: Eco, Sport, and Hardcore. Each mode adjusts the aggressiveness of the yaw rate target and the weighting of the QP objectives, letting the driver tune the car behavior from a dashboard switch without stopping. The controller was deployed and validated on the CAT16x for the 2023-2024 competition season.

FS — 02

Traction Control

Wheel slip management · CAT15x/16x

The traction control system is implemented as a speed controller running directly inside the inverter, rather than at the Processing Unit level. This is a deliberate architectural choice: the CAN-ROS communication link between the PU and the inverters runs at 50 Hz, which is too slow to react to wheel slip transients effectively. By pushing the control loop inside the inverter, which runs at a significantly higher frequency, the system responds to slip events before they can develop, without being bottlenecked by communication latency.

Inverter-level control loop architecture — speed controller block diagram

Beyond torque limiting, the system also enforces a velocity-based control using an optimal slip ratio target per wheel. Since competition surfaces are known and consistent, characterized during pre-season testing, the optimal operating point on the longitudinal force curve is determined in advance. The team modeled the Pacejka Magic Formula for their specific tires, giving a much more accurate peak slip ratio prediction than a generic model. The optimal slip ratio target is then adjusted as a function of slip angle per wheel, coupling the longitudinal and lateral tire behavior for a more complete slip management strategy.

FS — 03

Regenerative Braking

Energy recovery · CAT15x/16x

The regenerative braking system is designed around a single non-negotiable constraint: the ideal braking force distribution curve must always be maintained. Every mode and configuration operates within this envelope, ensuring vehicle stability and predictable brake bias regardless of how the electrical and mechanical forces are combined.

Ideal braking distribution curve — mechanical + electrical overlay

In performance-oriented scenarios such as AutoX, mechanical and electrical braking work in parallel from the first brake input. Hydraulic pressure sensors on both brake circuits measure the driver's braking demand in real time, allowing the system to calculate the current mechanical braking torque. Electrical braking torque is then adjusted to complement it precisely, giving the driver full pedal sensitivity while maximizing deceleration. The combined force distribution stays on the ideal curve throughout the braking event.

Parallel braking mode — hydraulic pressure sensor input to regen torque demand

When energy recovery is the priority, the system gives the driver an initial pedal travel handled entirely by the electric motors. Only when the electrical braking force is insufficient do the mechanical brakes engage, and both systems work in parallel again. This staging maximizes energy sent back to the battery on circuits where recovery matters more than peak deceleration, without the driver needing to change their braking technique.

Regen-first staging diagram — pedal travel zones and brake engagement

The system includes a wheel lockup prevention mechanism that compares individual wheel RPMs against the vehicle speed from the INS. If any wheel decelerates faster than physically consistent with the vehicle speed, electrical torque is reduced on that corner. A set of safety limits also monitors battery State of Charge and motor and inverter temperatures. If any threshold is exceeded, regenerative torque is progressively reduced with a smooth transition to pure mechanical braking so the driver is never caught off guard.

PRO — 01

CUPRA Exponential Experience

CUPRA Racing · Mixed Reality Racing on a CUPRA Raval

The CUPRA Exponential Experience is a first-of-its-kind mixed reality racing concept. A CUPRA Raval electric prototype is fitted with a full sensor suite, HMI system, a gaming PC and VR headsets. The driver physically drives the car around a parking lot while wearing VR glasses that overlay a virtual racetrack onto the real world. The virtual environment interacts with the real car in real time. If you hit a virtual obstacle, the car brakes. If you grab a boost power-up, you get real acceleration. There are lap counters, lap times, and competitive gameplay on top of actual driving.

I was responsible for programming and configuring the full software stack that connects the vehicle systems (CAN, sensors, actuators) to the virtual environment. This included designing the physical circuits (aerial layout, understanding both the videogame and real-world track characteristics and limitations), measuring and recording trajectories, and coordinating the game visuals with the external development company. On the positioning and HMI side, I coordinated and tested the software, found and fixed bugs and errors, and independently managed communication between two separate external companies. Finally, I built a real-time automation system that handled the full startup sequence, CAN checks, sensor validation, application coordination, and a safety layer that could record sessions and stop the car when dangerous situations were detected.

UNI — 03

Advanced Tyre Modelling and Testing

Vehicle Dynamics Lab · Politecnico di Milano

A laboratory study in Advanced Tyre Modelling and Testing at Politecnico di Milano, covering four interconnected areas of vehicle dynamics. The work spans steady-state cornering analysis with Pacejka Magic Formula parameter optimization, tyre thermal characterization under controlled slip angle inputs, sensitivity analysis of key tyre parameters on ABS performance, and lateral force characterization including relaxation length effects.

I worked through the full analysis pipeline across all four topics. On vehicle handling, I optimized front and rear cornering stiffness coefficients to achieve neutral attitude up to 5 m/s² lateral acceleration, quantifying the responsiveness-stability trade-off. On thermal testing, I characterized tyre temperature rise across different input waveforms, vertical loads, and speeds. The ABS study established clear sensitivity relationships between Pacejka parameters and braking performance, providing a structured picture of which tyre properties matter most under longitudinal slip.

UNI — 04

Optimal Vehicle Design

Vehicle Optimization Methods · Politecnico di Milano

A multi-topic optimization course at Politecnico di Milano that builds through eleven problem sets from classical gradient-based methods up to machine learning approaches, applied across vehicle subsystem design problems. Topics covered include braking force distribution, passive suspension tuning, suspension kinematics, vehicle lateral dynamics, and structural lightweight design of a lower control arm.

I worked across all eleven assignments, implementing solvers in MATLAB and applying the appropriate method to each problem: nonlinear minimization and Pareto optimization for multi-objective suspension trade-offs, genetic algorithms for suspension kinematics, artificial neural networks trained on VI-Grade simulation data for lateral dynamics prediction, and topology optimization in Altair Inspire for structural design. The project builds a practical understanding of when to use each method depending on problem dimensionality, continuity, and simulation cost.
